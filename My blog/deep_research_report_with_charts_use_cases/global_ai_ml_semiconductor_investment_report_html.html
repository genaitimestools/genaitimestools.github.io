Global Semiconductor Demand for AI/ML — Investment Report

Scope: historical 2019–2024 and projections for 2025–2030. Focus: AI/ML-driven demand across regions and sectors; company-level analysis; visualizations with Chart.js.

Executive Summary

This report examines the past five years (2019–2024) and the next five-year outlook (2025–2030) for semiconductor demand driven by AI/ML workloads. Key takeaways:

  • AI/ML workloads (training + inference) are the primary growth engine for semiconductors, producing a multi-year supercycle in demand.
  • Regional demand is concentrated in North America and Asia-Pacific, with North America leading due to hyperscaler datacenters and R&D activity.
  • Key growth sectors: data centers (training/inference), autonomous vehicles (ADAS/AV SoCs), and edge AI (smartphones, cameras, sensors, industrial edge).
  • Market value is highly concentrated: a small group of companies capture the majority of profits in AI semiconductors.

Sector Forecasts & Growth Drivers

We model three core sectors driving AI/ML semiconductor demand:

  • Data-center / Cloud HPC: GPUs, TPUs, AI accelerators and HBM memory for model training and inference clusters.
  • Autonomous Vehicles / ADAS: Vehicle AI SoCs, sensor fusion processors, and domain controllers.
  • Edge AI: On-device NPUs and inference accelerators in phones, cameras, industrial sensors and robotics.

Company-Level Analysis

Summary of leading companies and strategic positioning in AI/ML semiconductors:

  • NVIDIA: Leader in datacenter GPUs and AI ecosystem tools; strong pricing power and high gross margins from AI accelerators.
  • TSMC: Critical foundry partner for advanced nodes used by AI chip designers.
  • Intel & AMD: Competing for datacenter share with CPUs and accelerators; AMD gaining GPU traction.
  • Memory & Components (Samsung, Micron, SK Hynix, ASML): HBM and advanced process tools are essential to sustain AI performance growth.
  • Cloud Hyperscalers (Google, AWS, MSFT): Vertical integration (custom TPUs / Trainium / Inferentia) increases demand for specialized semiconductors.

Investment Implications

  1. Concentration of profits favors companies with IP, advanced process access, and ecosystem lock-in (e.g., NVIDIA, TSMC, ASML).
  2. Memory and advanced packaging suppliers are indirect but high-leverage plays (HBM, advanced substrates).
  3. Automotive and edge create diversified opportunities beyond cloud GPUs — look for suppliers with automotive-grade roadmaps and long-term OEM relationships.
  4. Geopolitical & policy risks (export controls, national semiconductor strategies) can create near-term volatility but also incentivize onshoring investments.
